<SectionHeading number="08">Risk Indicators</SectionHeading>

<Lead>Five risk readings in May's data warrant standalone treatment, separately from where each surfaces in the body. Two crossed thresholds this month. Three are continuations of patterns the report has been tracking since Issue 001, with the analytical clarity around each sharpened by what May's data added.</Lead>

Oracle concentration across the sector is 82.6 percent Chainlink at May 31, USD-weighted across the six protocols. Redstone is 7.4 percent, with the remaining 10 percent distributed across Pyth, Maker, Curve EMA, and an unmapped long tail of collateral assets that the report's classification scheme does not yet attribute. The unmapped bucket sits at roughly $1.80 billion of collateral, concentrated in the assets Compound V3 and Euler V2 brought into coverage this issue (XAUT, USDTB variants, PT tokens, smaller LRTs). Until that bucket is audited and assigned to specific oracle vendors, the true sector Chainlink share is likely high-80s to low-90s rather than the unaudited 82.6 percent. The Issue 001 figure of 94 percent should not be cited as a year-over-year drop until the audit is complete.

The per-protocol dispersion tells the structural story. Compound V3 sits at 93 percent Chainlink dependency, the most monoculture of the six, a consequence of the Comet design that standardizes collateral across one base asset per market. Aave V3 is 87 percent. Spark 86 percent. Fluid 77 percent. Morpho 67 percent. Euler V2 51 percent. The three protocols with vault-based architectures (Morpho, Euler V2, Fluid) are the most oracle-diversified, because each curator selects the oracle source they trust. The three protocols with pooled architectures (Compound V3, Aave V3, Spark) are the most concentrated.

<PullQuote>The three protocols with vault-based architectures are the most oracle-diversified. The three with pooled architectures are the most concentrated. Architecture determines oracle dependency more than risk preference does.</PullQuote>

LRT collateral across the sector contracted from $5.35 billion at April 30 to $3.73 billion at May 31, a $1.62 billion decline at actual prices. The constant-price net flow component was $1.17 billion; the remaining $443 million was LRT price decline across the month. weETH alone accounted for $1.18 billion of the actual-price contraction. By May 31, 88 percent of the LRT collateral remaining in the lending sector sat on Aave V3, with weETH at $2.12 billion, rsETH at $951 million, and osETH at $213 million on Aave V3 alone. Morpho held $246 million of weETH; the other four protocols carried negligible LRT exposure. The sector did not absorb the LRT rotation. LRT collateral stayed on Aave V3 even as its dollar value fell, while the depositor flows that exited rotated into BTC-family collateral and stables instead. The concentration was unchanged from April; what shifted in May was the dollar value.

Liquidation effective penalty across the trailing 90 days ending May 31 reads cleanly on three of the six protocols. Spark 4.87 percent. Morpho 24.10 percent. Fluid 1.68 percent. Fluid's penalty is the lowest in the sector by a wide margin and reflects the smart-collateral architecture documented in §06.4, where paired-asset positions with mathematically-managed price relationships reduce the slippage that drives penalty elsewhere. Morpho's 24.10 percent figure is the highest of the three, reflecting the long tail of risky-collateral isolated markets the protocol supports; while each Morpho market sets its own penalty, the trailing-90-day weighted average comes out higher because the markets that liquidate most often are also the ones with the highest configured penalties.

Aave V3's aggregate effective penalty came back at 261 percent across 911 events in the same trailing 90-day window. The figure is implausible and almost certainly an event-recording artifact in the underlying liquidator-economy database. One or two malformed rows where collateral seized was recorded as the full position value rather than the seized portion would produce this kind of outlier; alternatively, the indexer may be double-counting `LiquidationCall` events with their ancillary `Transfer` legs. Either way, the figure is not citeable as written and is under investigation. Compound V3 and Euler V2 are not yet ingested by the liquidator-economy database. For Euler V2 specifically, that gap is consequential: knowing whether the protocol's borrow contraction in May included forced liquidations would close the §06.6 borrow-runoff explanation. As written, that question remains open.

Curator concentration on Morpho is the structural risk §07 develops at length. The May 31 reading was HHI 3,103, the third consecutive month past the antitrust threshold for highly concentrated markets, with a June 4 follow-on capture at 3,290 confirming the trajectory had not stopped past the freeze. The top three curators (Sentora 38.6 percent, Steakhouse 34.1 percent, Gauntlet 21.2 percent) sit on 93.9 percent of curated TVL combined. The mechanism that §06.6's Sentora reallocation made empirically visible is that a single curator's decision can move $128 million between protocols in 30 days. That magnitude is the demonstrated discretionary surface. The risk implication for §08 specifically: any one of the three top curators choosing to reallocate at similar scale would move sector-significant capital between protocols inside a single capture window.

One cross-protocol reading warrants flagging here as well. The cross-protocol USDC supply APY dispersion sat at 330.5 basis points, 1.33 times the trailing 12-month average, with Fluid USDC paying 5.93 percent at the top of the range against Euler V2 USDC at 2.62 percent at the bottom. USDT and WETH dispersion both ran below their 12-month baselines this month; only USDC stayed elevated. The risk is not any single market defaulting; it is the persistence of asset-specific dispersion above baseline being itself the proximate mechanism of the §03 macro story, with the sector's most-deposited stablecoin running the widest rate book.

Of the five readings above, two crossed thresholds in May for the first time: the Real Yield Spread closed at parity (covered in §03) and the sector take rate fell below the 4-week T-bill (covered in §04). The other three are continuations of patterns the report has been tracking since Issue 001, with the analytical clarity around each sharpened by May's data. The curator concentration story moved from static observation to demonstrated discretionary magnitude. The LRT contraction shifted from category-wide to specifically-Aave-V3-concentrated. The oracle concentration restated downward, subject to the unmapped audit. The liquidations picture remains incomplete pending data quality fixes. None of the five is a crisis indicator. All five are structural conditions whose June trajectories shape what the next issue carries.
