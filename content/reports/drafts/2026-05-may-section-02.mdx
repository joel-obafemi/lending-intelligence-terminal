<SectionHeading number="02">Executive Summary</SectionHeading>

<Lead>May was the month one curator restructured their cross-protocol footprint inside thirty days, moving $128 million out of one lending protocol and $94 million into another with no flagged event behind the timing.</Lead>

The operator was Sentora. The protocols were Euler V2 and Morpho. The move was visible only because the same name appears in the top-curator slots of both. No oracle break, no governance vote, no liquidation cascade. The mechanism was operator discretion at a magnitude this publication has not previously been able to quantify with attribution. Roughly three quarters of the Euler outflow reappeared as Sentora-curated inflow on Morpho in the same window; the remaining quarter was downstream LP behavior we ruled out three obvious alternative explanations for, leaving distributed depositor decisions as the most likely residual.

The Sentora reallocation is one half of a larger pattern. Morpho's curator HHI rose for the third consecutive month, from 3,026 at the April close to 3,103 at the May close, deepening past the antitrust threshold for highly concentrated markets. Sentora is the largest of the three curators occupying that concentration, and their Morpho footprint grew $93.94M during the month, almost all of which came from their own Euler reallocation. The HHI deepening was partly the largest curator moving capital onto their own pile. By June 4, the first programmatic capture of the next month's reading showed HHI at 3,290, up another 187 points in four days, with Sentora's share moving from 38.6 percent to 41.3 percent. The Sentora-to-Morpho reallocation had not stopped.

Beyond the curator story, the LRT collateral base across the sector continued to drain in May, with roughly $1.17B of net outflow across weETH, rsETH, ezETH, and osETH. The procedural moments that received the most attention (rsETH's May 14 unpause and the May 17 WETH LTV restoration on Aave V3) did not gate the exit at the asset level. Daily flow data shows weETH-specific outflows on Aave V3 were larger in the first half of the month than the second: −$459M between May 1 and May 13, against −$222M between May 14 and May 31. The LRT exit was continuous through the month rather than triggered by the procedural unfreeze. The post-unpause outflow spikes that did occur on Aave V3 were dominated by stables and other collateral, not LRTs.

The headline supply contraction on Aave V3 is more measurement than movement. Total supply fell $1.96B at nominal prices, but at constant prices the protocol gained $352M of net deposits across the month. The wedge between the two readings is price decline on existing LRT and ETH-family collateral. Constant-price accounting tells the depositor-flow story; nominal accounting captures the mark-to-market effect of falling collateral prices. Both readings are correct from their own definitions, and both are worth holding in view: the depositor base on Aave V3 grew in May while the dollar value of what they hold shrank.

The Real Yield Spread, the gap between blended stablecoin lending APY across the six protocols and the 4-week US Treasury bill, closed May at parity for the first month-end since September 2025. The May 31 reading was −0.3 basis points, effectively zero, against −34 basis points at the April close. The 33-basis-point tightening is the largest single-month compression of 2026 and closes a four-month rally from a February trough at −151 basis points. Stable APYs lifted to match T-bills at 3.60 percent rather than T-bill yields falling, the same recovery mechanism that played out in April. The 34-basis-point lift distributed across the protocols rather than concentrating in any one market, with Aave V3 USDC closing at 3.27 percent supply APY against Fluid USDC's 5.93 percent at the top of the range and Euler V2 USDC's 2.62 percent at the bottom. Cross-protocol USDC dispersion at 330.5 basis points still ran 33 percent above its 12-month baseline, even after substantial easing from April's spike.

A related observation surfaces in the sector take rate, the same comparison from a different angle. Trailing-30-day Ethereum-only fees annualized against available liquidity fell from 4.27 percent at April 30 to 3.38 percent at May 31, an 89-basis-point compression. Available liquidity barely moved across the month, down 0.7 percent; the entire decline came from fees, which fell 19.7 percent sector-wide and concentrated almost entirely in Aave V3. The May 31 take rate of 3.38 percent now sits below the 4-week T-bill yield of 3.60 percent, the first month-end at which the sector's annualized revenue yield has run below the risk-free rate in the captured series. The Real Yield Spread at parity tells the same story from the depositor side. The take rate compression tells it from the protocol side. Both readings deliver the same observation: lending capital across the six protocols this report covers no longer pays a premium over Treasuries.

Coverage this issue expands from four protocols to six, adding Compound V3 and Euler V2 to the Aave V3, Spark, Morpho, and Fluid baseline carried from Issue 001. Both new protocols are smaller than the original four in absolute supply terms (Compound V3 at $1.61B Ethereum supply, Euler V2 at $549M), but together they add roughly $2.15B to a $33B sector. The expansion changes how month-over-month comparisons read. The six-protocol April 30 baseline is $34.64B against the four-protocol $32.26B published in Issue 001. The sector contracted 4.6 percent on the honest six-protocol comparison, not the +1.3 percent that a naïve juxtaposition of the two baselines would imply.

Three risk readings stand out across the issue. The first is the Morpho idle ratio, which stood at 63.7 percent at the May 31 close. That means roughly sixty-three cents of every dollar in Morpho's vault economy is sitting unallocated, a signal that curators are holding back from deploying into markets they don't trust. The second is the stablecoin debt share, which rebounded from 43.6 percent at the April close to 59.3 percent at the May close, driven by LRT loops repaying WETH debt and rotating into stablecoin borrowing. The composition shift is large enough to merit attention regardless of the Sentora-specific finding. The third is methodological: Aave V3's aggregate liquidation effective penalty came back at 261 percent in our 90-day rollup, almost certainly an event-recording artifact in the underlying database. Spark, Morpho, and Fluid penalties are in-range; the Aave figure is footnoted in §08 pending investigation. Compound V3 and Euler V2 are not yet ingested by the liquidator-economy database, and for Euler V2 specifically that gap matters for how May's borrow contraction reads in light of the Sentora finding.
